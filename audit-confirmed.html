Before the audit

Five things to have ready before I arrive.

None of these are blockers — if any aren't possible, the audit still happens. But the more of this you can have lined up, the more we'll get done on the day.

1

Confirmed access

Reception/security informed I'm arriving. Visitor pass arranged. Hi-vis available if your site requires it. If there's a site induction, scheduled for first thing.

2

Key people on-site

Whoever you said would be available in the enquiry form — operations director or warehouse manager, a shift supervisor, ideally a senior operative. I'll need 20–30 minutes with each, ideally during their normal shift so I can see them in context.

3

Operational data

If accessible: the last 3 months of pick rate, labour cost, overtime, and goods-in throughput. Doesn't need to be pretty — raw exports are fine. I'll review during quiet moments on the day rather than expecting a formal report.

4

Site documentation (optional)

Any process maps, SOPs, layout drawings, or recent improvement-project reports. Not essential, but they accelerate the diagnosis and let me ask sharper questions of your team.

5

An honest brief

Before the audit, I'll send a short call to confirm logistics and ask for context I won't get on the day — political sensitivities, who's struggling, what's been tried, what's off-limits. Honesty here directly improves what I can find.

On the day

A typical audit day, in four blocks.

Most audits follow this shape. Exact timing varies by site — multi-shift operations sometimes run longer to capture both shifts; single-shift sites tend to compress.

08:00 – 10:00

Floor walk & observation

Start on the floor before any meetings. Watch how the day actually begins. Note: shift handover quality, putaway flow, picker behaviour, supervisor patterns, KPI board activity. Most of the audit's value comes from the first two hours.

10:00 – 13:00

Interviews & process mapping

One-on-one conversations with each of the key people identified in the enquiry. 20–30 minutes each. Process mapping the critical flows: goods-in, putaway, picking, despatch, returns. Document what's actually done, not what the SOP says.

13:00 – 16:00

Data review & cross-checking

Sit with your operational data. Validate observations against the numbers. Test hypotheses: if I think handover is broken, does the data show the second shift's slow start? If I think slotting drifted, does the SKU velocity report support it?

16:00 – 17:00

Verbal debrief

One-hour debrief with you (and anyone you want present). High-level findings, top three risk areas, things I want to verify in writing. Not the final report — but enough to know if you'll find the written findings useful, and what you should brief your team on tomorrow.

After the audit

Within 7 working days, you'll have your written report.

The written findings report goes deeper than the verbal debrief — every observation is documented with evidence, severity rating, and recommended action. Quick wins are flagged separately so your team can begin while strategic recommendations are scoped.
